## Abstract 

Reinforcement learning (RL) for large language models usually supervises reasoning with scalar outcome rewards, such as binary correctness. Such rewards provide an optimization direction but rarely explain how a model should revise its mistaken reasoning, which can encourage shortcut learning and brittle policies. We propose SocraticPO (Socratic Policy Optimization), a policy-optimization framework that augments RL rollouts with Socratic-style natural-language guidance. During rollout, the student first answers independently; if the answer is incorrect, a teacher diagnoses the attempt and provides concise corrective guidance, after which the student continues under the expanded context. Crucially, this guidance is paired with reward decay: correct answers obtained after teacher intervention only receive decayed rewards, preventing the policy from treating teacher help as a free path to reward. Since SocraticPO only modifies the rollout process while leaving the standard expected-reward objective intact, it can be plugged into existing policy-gradient backends such as Reinforce++. Moreover, because the teacher provides only text-level guidance, SocraticPO can leverage stronger black-box teacher models without requiring access to logits or distribution matching. On undergraduate-level scientific reasoning benchmarks from SciKnowEval, SocraticPO improves over strong RL and self-distillation baselines. Ablations show that both targeted guidance and reward decay are necessary, with reward decay mitigating reliance on assisted correction.

## 1 Introduction 

Large language models (LLMs) have advanced rapidly with transformer architectures, scaling, and instruction tuning [ 1, 3, 5, 26 , 29 ]. Their post-training increasingly relies on reinforcement learning (RL), where a policy is optimized from task rewards or preference-derived signals [ 6, 18 , 23 ]. For reasoning tasks, however, RL feedback is often reduced to a scalar outcome signal, such as binary correctness. Such feedback tells the model which trajectories should be reinforced, but not how a mistaken reasoning process should be repaired. This limitation is especially problematic in multi-step scientific reasoning. A wrong final answer may arise from a localized conceptual error, an incorrect comparison, or a misplaced intermediate value. Outcome rewards provide gradient-level optimization pressure, but they do not provide thought-level correction. As a result, the policy may discover shortcuts that satisfy the verifier without learning robust reasoning. 1

Human learning offers a different pattern of supervision. Teachers rarely provide only a grade; they inspect the learner’s attempt, identify the source of the mistake, and give hints, critiques, or next-step suggestions [ 28 , 33 ]. The Socratic tradition frames this process as guided inquiry rather than direct answer transfer [ 16 , 20 ]. Inspired by this view, we introduce Socratic Policy Optimization (SocraticPO) , which brings Socratic-style guidance into the rollout stage of RL. A student first attempts the problem independently. If the attempt is incorrect, a teacher provides natural-language guidance conditioned on the student’s response, and the student continues under the expanded context. Guidance must be introduced carefully. If a response corrected after teacher intervention receives the same reward as one solved independently, the policy may learn a training-time shortcut: wait for help, use the teacher-provided clue, and still collect full reward. Assisted correctness can then improve without a corresponding improvement in unassisted reasoning. SocraticPO therefore pairs teacher guidance with reward decay : correct answers obtained after teacher intervention receive decayed rewards, and the reward decreases as more guidance rounds are needed. This encourages the student not only to reach the correct answer, but also to internalize the correction and solve earlier with less assistance. SocraticPO changes only the rollout process. The student still optimizes a standard expected-reward objective, while teacher utterances are appended as text context for subsequent student attempts rather than optimized as imitation targets. As a result, SocraticPO can be instantiated with existing policy-gradient backends such as Reinforce++ [ 12 ]. Since the teacher is required only to produce text-level guidance, the framework can also leverage stronger black-box teacher models without accessing logits, token-level likelihoods, or matching teacher and student distributions, unlike probability-matching approaches such as OPD [2, 9]. We evaluate SocraticPO on undergraduate-level scientific reasoning benchmarks derived from SciKnowEval [ 8]. Compared with strong RL baselines, including Reinforce++ [ 12 ] and GRPO [ 21 ], as well as self-distillation-style training such as SDPO [ 14 ], SocraticPO achieves the best average performance. Ablations further show that both components are necessary: targeted guidance provides useful correction beyond blind retrying, while reward decay mitigates reliance on teacher-assisted correction. Our main contributions are: 

• A general SocraticPO framework. We propose an interactive policy-optimization framework that augments RL with teacher-guided reasoning correction and remains compatible with existing policy-gradient backends. 

• Reward decay for SocraticPO. To make teacher-guided RL train the student rather than reward dependence on assistance, we introduce a reward decay mechanism for assisted correctness. We further characterize its normalized-advantage properties, showing non-negativity, monotonicity, and saturation decay. 

• Black-box teacher compatibility. Our method only requires teachers to provide text-level guidance instead of distribution matching, making it compatible with stronger API-based or otherwise black-box teachers. 2• Empirical evidence and analysis. We show gains on scientific reasoning benchmarks and provide ablations on teacher guidance, reward decay, teacher-side information, and teacher–student similarity. 

## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Reinforcement Learning for LLMs 

Reinforcement learning has become a central paradigm for aligning LLMs with human preferences and task rewards [ 6, 18 , 23 , 36 ]. PPO [ 19 ] is widely used in RLHF pipelines, and feedback can also be provided by AI systems rather than direct human annotation [ 4]. More recent LLM training methods adopt simpler or more scalable policy-gradient variants, including REINFORCE-style updates [ 12 , 24 , 32 ] and group-wise relative objectives such as GRPO [21]. Most of these methods optimize scalar reward signals. In reasoning tasks, such rewards identify whether a trajectory should be reinforced but do not explain which part of the reasoning process should be corrected. Process-supervision work shows that intermediate feedback can be more informative than outcome supervi-sion alone [ 27 ], yet how to inject corrective information into on-policy RL rollouts remains underexplored. SocraticPO addresses this gap by adding natural-language correction to rollout sampling while retaining a standard policy-gradient objective. 

2.2 Human Guided Teaching 

Educational research emphasizes that effective teaching is interactive rather than purely evaluative. Tutors scaffold learning by inspecting a student’s current attempt, diagnosing misconceptions, and providing hints or next-step guidance [ 28 , 33 ]. The Socratic tradition similarly frames learning as guided inquiry: questions and critiques help the learner revise an answer without simply copying an expert solution [ 20 ]. Recent NLP work has explored Socratic tutoring and personalized teaching dialogues with LLMs [16]. SocraticPO adapts this teaching principle to RL training. The teacher does not merely label an answer as wrong; it provides language-level correction conditioned on the student’s failed attempt. At the same time, SocraticPO differs from tutoring systems whose goal is to optimize dialogue quality. Our goal is to improve the student policy through RL, so teacher guidance is paired with a reward design that encourages the student to solve earlier and rely less on future intervention. 

2.3 Student--Teacher Paradigms in LLMs 

Teacher–student learning is widely used in LLM post-training to transfer capability from stronger models to smaller or weaker students. One common paradigm is teacher-generated supervised fine-tuning (SFT), where a stronger model produces instructions, answers, or reasoning traces that are then treated as demonstration data for the student. Self-Instruct, Alpaca, WizardLM, Orca, and step-by-step distillation all follow this broad pattern [ 11 , 17 , 25 , 31 , 34 ]. Recent work also studies how to synthesize student-consistent SFT data from a teacher so that the demonstrations better match the student’s learning state [13]. Another paradigm is knowledge or policy distillation, where the student is trained to approximate a teacher’s behavior more directly. Classical distillation matches teacher distributions or soft targets [ 10 ], while on-policy distillation adapts this idea to LLMs by using model-generated or teacher-shaped trajectories during training [ 2, 9, 22 ]. Self-distillation and hybrid distillation variants further use the model’s own successful responses, privileged trajectories, or routed samples as training targets [ 7, 14 , 15 , 35 ]. Despite differences in data source and objective, these methods generally ask the student to imitate, approximate, or absorb teacher-like behavior. SocraticPO adopts a different teacher–student relation. The teacher is not a demonstration generator whose answer should be copied, nor a distributional target whose probabilities should be matched. Instead, it acts as an interactive guide: after a failed student attempt, the teacher diagnoses the mistake and provides concise corrective guidance, while the student still produces its own subsequent response and is optimized only through task reward. Because this guidance is text-level, SocraticPO can also use black-box teachers as an additional benefit of the design. 33 Preliminaries

## 4 Method 

Traditional scalar rewards only tell the model whether an answer is correct or incorrect. They provide an optimization signal, but not a diagnosis of the reasoning error that caused the failure. In human learning, a teacher often plays this diagnostic role: by pointing out where the student’s current attempt goes wrong, the teacher can help the student revise more efficiently than a grade alone. SocraticPO turns this idea into a policy-optimization mechanism. It modifies the rollout stage by inserting conditional teacher feedback after failed student attempts, so that the policy learns from trajectories that contain both the original mistake and a correction-oriented context for the next attempt. 44.1 Interactive Rollout

The first property shows that SocraticPO does not punish corrected reasoning. Once a sample is answered correctly, the corresponding student tokens receive a non-negative normalized advantage, even if the answer is obtained after teacher guidance. Thus, reward decay does not discard assisted successes ; it keeps them as valid positive learning signals. The second property formalizes the batch-level credit assignment discussed above. Among samples that eventually become correct within the same batch, SocraticPO gives a larger normalized advantage to those corrected at earlier steps. This prevents an independently solved problem and a problem solved only after multiple rounds of guidance from receiving indistinguishable credit. In this sense, reward decay calibrates positive reward across samples by favoring trajectories that become correct with fewer guidance rounds .The third property explains how reward decay mitigates assisted reward hacking. If teacher guidance makes almost all active samples correct, then pk → 1 and the advantage approaches zero. Once a guidance round becomes non-discriminative because nearly everyone can solve the task with that amount of help, trajectories from that round no longer provide strong positive reinforcement. This means that reward decay can prevent the student policy from gaining excessive advantage merely by relying on teacher intervention .

5.4 Additional Analysis 

Teacher access to ground truth. We study whether the teacher should receive ground-truth answers. Although such access seems useful for generating precise feedback, Figure 5 shows that it can degrade student performance in most cases. We attribute this to two factors. First, even when the prompt explicitly asks the teacher not to reveal the answer, a teacher with ground-truth access may still leak solution-specific hints. This failure mode is inherently limited by the teacher model’s instruction-following ability. Second, ground-truth access can shift the teacher from diagnosing the student’s reasoning process to reverse-engineering hints from the known answer. Both effects may encourage shortcut learning rather than robust correction. At the same time, ground-truth information is not intrinsically harmful. If the teacher can avoid answer leakage and use the reference solution to diagnose the student’s error, then access to the answer or even a standard reasoning process can substantially improve the quality of guidance, as suggested by the Qwen3-8B-Instruct self-guidance result on Chemistry. We use Qwen3-8B as the teacher and Qwen3-4B-Instruct as the student on the Material benchmark. This pair is chosen because KL computation requires aligned to-kenization, and the two models share a compatible tokenizer. If SocraticPO were primarily a distillation mechanism, we would expect teacher–student divergence to decrease as train-ing proceeds. Figure 6 shows that SocraticPO does not con-sistently reduce the KL divergence between teacher and student, even though it improves downstream task performance. This supports the distinction made in our method: the teacher participates in rollout as an interactive guide, but the student is not optimized to reproduce the teacher’s token distribution or reasoning trace. In other words, SocraticPO improves the student through guided correction rather than by making the student become more teacher-like. Together, these analyses suggest that effective teaching protocols require more than a stronger teacher. The information exposed to the teacher, the form of feedback, and the training objective jointly determine whether interaction improves policy learning. 

10 6 Conclusion 

We presented SocraticPO , a policy-optimization framework that addresses a limitation of scalar reward feedback in LLM reinforcement learning. Outcome rewards indicate whether a response should be reinforced, but they do not explain how a mistaken reasoning process should be repaired. SocraticPO augments rollout sampling with natural-language teacher guidance: the student first attempts a solution, receives targeted correction only after an incorrect response, and continues under the expanded dialogue context. Teacher guidance must be controlled carefully. If assisted correct answers are rewarded as strongly as independent correct answers, the student may exploit teacher intervention instead of improving its own reasoning. SocraticPO therefore pairs guidance with reward decay, assigning smaller rewards to answers that become correct only after more interaction rounds. Experiments on SciKnowEval scientific reasoning benchmarks show that SocraticPO improves average performance over strong RL and self-distillation baselines, while ablations highlight the importance of both targeted guidance and reward decay. Our findings suggest that natural-language correction can serve as process-level supervision inside RL without requiring teacher distribution matching. Because the teacher is used only as a text-producing guide, SocraticPO can leverage black-box mentors and remains modular with respect to the underlying RL optimizer. Future work can further study teaching protocols, teacher-side information, and broader tasks where reasoning errors are diagnosable before the final answer.
