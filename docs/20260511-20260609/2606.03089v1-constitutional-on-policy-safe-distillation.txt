## Abstract 

On-policy self-distillation (OPSD) has emerged as an efficient post-training paradigm by using a teacher conditioned on privileged information to provide dense token-level supervision. Prior work has shown that OPSD can collapse in verifiable reasoning tasks, but safety alignment differs in that it is guided by high-level constitutions rather than explicit target answers, making it a natural setting to revisit dense distillation. However, our pilot study show that safety OPSD still suffers from severe collapse: constitutional conditioning contracts the teacher distribution toward short and overly conservative responses, and Reverse KL further amplifies this contraction into reduced expressiveness. We formalize this effect as geometric leakage under safety boundaries in a non-orthogonal semantic space, where safety pressure transfers into the expressiveness dimension. Based on this analysis, we propose Constitutional On-Policy Safe Distillation (COPSD) , which first calibrates the teacher through a Cross-SFT cold-start and then performs constitution-conditioned on-policy distillation. Experiments on 12 benchmarks show that COPSD achieves a consistently stronger safety–helpfulness trade-off than baselines while substantially reducing the safety tax on general reasoning ability.

## 1 Introduction 

On-Policy Self-Distillation (OPSD) [ 17 , 40 , 47 ] efficiently aligns foundation models by utilizing a temporary teacher augmented with privileged information to generate token-level reinforcement learning rewards. Despite its empirical success, recent studies have identified a severe vulnerability of this framework in reasoning tasks, manifested as abruptly truncated generations and catastrophic distributional collapse [ 9, 20 ]. This degradation arises because the student exploits superficial correlations between the teacher’s privileged cues and the final output, rather than internalizing the underlying reasoning process [ 39 ]. As a result, the student minimizes the divergence penalty by collapsing onto low-entropy shortcuts that are statistically dominant but logically deficient. Safety alignment appears to be a particularly promising setting for OPSD for two reasons. First, unlike reasoning tasks, safety tasks typically lack explicit reference answers and instead rely on high-level consti-tutions or principles [ 5 , 10 , 42 ]. Conditioning the teacher on such principles may encourage the student to internalize genuine safety boundaries, rather than exploit answer-specific shortcuts. Second, mitigating 1

OPSD’s dense token-level supervision offers a potentially suitable mechanism for providing the fine-grained calibration needed to navigate this trade-off. From this perspective, the safety domain provides a natural setting for examining whether OPSD can deliver efficient alignment without inducing distributional collapse. To evaluate this hypothesis, we study standard OPSD and three variants on the Qwen3 model family [ 3 ], including token-level credit assignment and entropy-aware regularization. However, even in the absence of answer-based shortcuts, the student still exhibits severe response truncation and distributional collapse. Our pilot study in Section 3 reveals that this failure does not arise from shortcut recitation: the lexical overlap between privileged information and generated responses remains consistently low across variants. Instead, the collapse originates from the constitution-conditioned teacher itself. Once conditioned on safety constitution, the teacher distribution contracts into a manifold of short, cautious responses, with substantial reductions in entropy, response length, and generative diversity. To explain this observation more precisely, Section 3.3 models the constitution-conditioned teacher as an Energy-Based distribution composed of an intrinsic linguistic prior and an external safety potential. We analyze the induced update dynamics in a non-orthogonal semantic subspace whose two principal directions correspond to safety and expressiveness.Unlike verifiable domains, where privileged information specifies an explicit target to be reached, safety constitutions primarily define a boundary to avoid. Because these two directions are geometrically coupled, the gradient leaks into the expressiveness dimension, producing a passive contraction of response length, entropy, and diversity. This perspective explains why safety OPSD collapses even without shortcut copying: the model is not pulled toward a better target response, but pushed away from unsafe regions in a geometry where safety pressure spills over into expressive behavior. Building on this diagnosis, we propose COPSD (Constitutional On-Policy Safe Distillation), a two-stage framework designed to reduce the geometric entanglement between safety and expressiveness. In the first stage, a Cross-SFT Cold-Start calibrates the teacher on a mixed corpus of constitution-guided and expressiveness-preserving trajectories, so as to better anchor the linguistic prior under safety constitution. In the second stage, the recalibrated teacher is integrated into an on-policy distillation pipeline, where it provides dense token-level supervision on the student’s rollouts under constitutional conditioning. Extensive experiments on 12 benchmarks show that COPSD achieves a consistently better safety–helpfulness trade-off than state-of-the-art alignment baselines such as GRPO and OPD, while preserving general capabilities. In summary, our main contributions are as follows: 

• We show that OPSD fails differently in safety alignment than in verifiable reasoning tasks, with the latter collapsing toward explicit target response patterns and the former collapsing through boundary-induced geometric leakage into expressiveness under a non-orthogonal representation geometry. 

• We propose COPSD, a two-stage framework that uses Cross-SFT Cold-Start to calibrate the teacher and mitigate safety–expressiveness entanglement prior to constitution-conditioned on-policy distillation. 2• Experiments across 12 benchmarks show that COPSD achieves a consistently stronger safety–helpfulness trade-off than competitive baselines, while substantially reducing the safety tax on general capabilities. 

## 2 Related Work 

On-policy distillation. Reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards (RLVR) [ 11 , 15 , 48 ] has emerged as a prominent approach for post-training large language models. Using algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [ 29 ] and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [ 31 ], RLVR significantly enhances reasoning performance in domains such as mathematics and coding. However, RLVR is fundamentally constrained by sparse sequence-level supervision: rewards are typically assigned only to the final response, providing little token-level feedback on exactly where the generation succeeds or fails [ 12 , 46 ]. To address this, recent work explores on-policy distillation [ 1, 19 , 21 , 23 ], which trains on the student’s own rollouts while replacing sparse outcome rewards with dense, token-level supervision from a teacher. A notable variant is on-policy self-distillation (OPSD) [ 17 , 40 , 47 ], where the same model acts as both teacher and student under varying contexts, improving token efficiency without relying on an external teacher. Nevertheless, recent analyses [ 20 ] indicate that self-distillation with a richer teacher context can inadvertently suppress epistemic verbalization, encouraging overly compressed and overconfident reasoning that degrades out-of-distribution generalization. Furthermore, existing OPSD frameworks have predominantly been explored in text-only reasoning tasks, leaving a critical gap in multimodal grounding. 

Safety Alignment Safety alignment ensures that models adhere to human values, with SFT, ICL, and RLHF being the most prominent paradigms [6, 8, 28 , 35 ]. These approaches share a fundamental limitation: they provide only sequence-level sparse supervision that cannot distinguish which tokens contribute to safety versus helpfulness, forcing the model to navigate this trade-off at a coarse granularity and leading to over-refusal or residual unsafety [ 4 , 36 , 37 ]. Constitutional approaches attempt to provide denser guidance by conditioning a teacher on safety principles [ 5], yet this exposes a deeper issue: the safety and helpfulness objectives are geometrically coupled in the representation space, causing the conditioned teacher to collapse onto low-diversity, over-cautious outputs. Existing paradigms thus face a dilemma: they either lack token-level precision or trigger distributional collapse when attempting to provide it [ 39 ]. COPSD resolves this dilemma by first decoupling the competing objectives via Cross-SFT, then performing on-policy distillation on the recalibrated teacher. 

## 3 Distributional Collapse in Safety OPSD: A Pilot Study 

We conduct a pilot study on a 9K-sample multi-objective dataset designed to balance safety alignment and general capability preservation. The dataset includes safety-oriented subsets (2K BeaverTails-V [ 18 ], 2K SPA-VL [ 45 ], and 1K MM-RLHF [ 44 ] Safety), general-domain data (2K MMPR [ 34 ] and 1K MM-RLHF General), and 300 curated over-refusal queries. Using Qwen3-VL-4B and the VERL framework, we train for three epochs, incorporating the outcome reward design from Pragma-VL [ 36 ] to provide verifier feedback during reinforcement learning. The safety evaluation spans general safety (BeaverTails, SPA-VL, and VLGuard [ 50 ]), situational safety (SIUO [ 33 ] and MSS-Bench [ 49 ]), and semantic hazard mitigation (VLSBench [ 13 ]), using win-rates against the base model for BeaverTails and SPA-VL and evaluator-based absolute scores for the remaining benchmarks. This suggests that the collapse observed in safety alignment is not caused by shortcut copying from privileged information, but instead by the way constitutional conditioning reshapes the target distribution itself. 

3.2 Empirical Diagnosis: Entanglement within the Linguistic Prior 

To understand these failures, we examine how the constitution-conditioned teacher interacts with the choice of KL divergence. As shown in Figure 2, constitutional conditioning shifts the teacher toward shorter, less diverse, and more refusal-like outputs. Standard OPSD then amplifies this contraction through Reverse KL (Eq. 1), which strongly penalizes tokens outside the teacher’s support. As a result, the student is pushed toward a compressed short-and-safe pattern, leading to shorter answers, less explanation, and lower helpfulness. This suggests that safety pressure does not stay confined to refusal behavior, but also spills over into the model’s expressiveness. Entropy-Aware OPSD tries to counter this effect by adding a Forward KL term in high-entropy regions. This helps preserve diversity, but it also weakens the sharp exclusion effect needed for robust safety control. As a result, the model remains more expressive, yet fails to enforce strong constitutional constraints. ## 4 Constitutional On-Policy Safe Distillation 

Section 3 shows that safety OPSD fails because that constitutional conditioning pushes the teacher toward overly short and conservative responses, which are further amplified by Reverse KL. This motivates Constitutional On-Policy Safe Distillation (COPSD) , a two-stage framework that recalibrates the teacher through Cross-SFT and then performs constitution-conditioned on-policy distillation.

5.3 Ablation Study 

Impact of Teacher SFT Epochs. We first investigate the sensitivity of COPSD to the SFT training duration of the teacher model. As illustrated in Figure 7, while a 2-epoch SFT teacher ( T-2ep ) serves as a robust foundation, further increasing training to 5 epochs ( T-5ep )leads to a significant degradation in overall performance. Our analysis in Appendix C.3 reveals that excessive SFT causes the model to overfit to specific generative metrics, such as response length, at the cost of semantic entropy and response diversity. This over-optimization results in a catastrophic collapse of safety scores (e.g., dropping from 77 .76% to 55 .69% on BeaverTails) and effectiveness, confirming that teacher training duration must be carefully moderated to prevent the "collapse of diversity" in downstream distillation. 

Importance of Self-Generated Data Distribution. We further examine the role of data composition by removing self-generated trajectories from the SFT ( T-w/o self ). While this variant maintains competitive safety scores on par with the standard COPSD, it suffers a decline across nearly all general capability benchmarks. For instance, MathVista performance drops from 72 .70% to 64 .60% . This phenomenon highlights a subtle form of alignment tax : without the inclusion of self-generated distributions, the model tends to over-fit to external safety patterns that may be geometrically misaligned with its internal reasoning manifolds. This optimization conflict forces the model to sacrifice general reasoning utility to meet safety constraints, underscoring the necessity of self-generated data in maintaining a coherent generative footprint during alignment. 12 Safety Score

## 6 Conclusion 

This paper studies on-policy self-distillation in safety alignment and identifies a distinct collapse mechanism from that in verifiable tasks. We show that safety OPSD collapses because constitutional conditioning contracts the teacher distribution toward short and overly conservative responses, and Reverse KL amplifies this contraction into reduced expressiveness. We formalize this as geometric leakage under safety boundaries and propose COPSD, a two-stage framework that combines a Cross-SFT cold-start with constitution-conditioned on-policy distillation. ## B Training Details 

B.1 SFT Data Curation 

The SFT data follows the same overall composition as the training corpus described in the main text, including safety-oriented samples from BeaverTails-V, SPA-VL, and MM-RLHF Safety, general-domain samples from MMPR and MM-RLHF General, and a small set of over-refusal queries. For general-domain data, including MMPR and MM-RLHF General, we use the policy model to generate responses under the default constitution policy. This branch is used to preserve the base model’s native response style under constitutional conditioning. For safety-related data, we query multiple frontier models, including Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B-Instruct , GPT-5.4 , and Gemini 3.1 , with the corresponding constitutional prompts. Their responses are then compared by an LLM judge in a pairwise manner, and the selected responses are required to be both safer and more effective. For over-refusal data, we leverage the multimodal scenario taxonomy and pipeline from CSR-Bench [ 22 ] to construct paired descriptions of text queries and corresponding image contents. Specifically, for each pair, the text query is designed to appear risky when read in isolation, while the paired image description specifies visual content that, when combined with the text query, renders the full input clearly benign under cross-modal understanding. We then generate the corresponding images from these image descriptions using the Seedream 4.5 text-to-image model, followed by manual quality inspection to ensure that each image-text pair is genuinely safe under joint interpretation while remaining deceptive in the text-only modality. The selected frontier responses are subsequently rewritten by the policy model to better match the policy model’s own style. We further resample these rewritten responses to approximately match the original response-length distribution of the policy model on the same queries. This procedure produces a mixed SFT corpus that strengthens safety supervision while preserving expressive characteristics. 20 B.2 RL Training Details 

We implement RL training with the VERL framework. For both Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct, we train for 3 epochs with a global batch size of 128. The maximum prompt length and maximum response length are both set to 2048. The actor learning rate is set to 2 × 10 −6, the PPO mini-batch size is 32, and the micro-batch size per GPU is 8. Rollouts are generated with vLLM using stochastic sampling, with 16 rollouts per prompt. For the standard COPSD variant, token-level rewards are computed from the on-policy log-probability discrepancy between the student and the constitution-conditioned teacher. For the hybrid variant, we combine the token-level signal with an outcome-level reward as in Eq. 23, and set 𝜆 =0 .3. All other hyperparameters remain unchanged. 

B.3 Baseline Training Details 

We train OPD, GRPO, and Safe-RLHF-V under the same basic optimization setup as COPSD whenever applicable. All baselines are trained for 3 epochs with a global batch size of 128, maximum prompt and response lengths of 2048, PPO mini-batch size 32, micro-batch size 8 per GPU, actor learning rate 2 × 10 −6,and KL loss coefficient 0.005 with the low-variance KL formulation. We use stochastic rollout generation with 16 samples per prompt and disable explicit entropy regularization. For GRPO, we use the standard grpo 

advantage estimator and replace the token-level distillation reward with an external reward model. The reward model is trained based on [ 36 ], a multi-modal reward model that provides both helpfulness and safety scores. Safe-RLHF-V is trained with the same reward-model framework as GRPO, except that we enable the self-RLHF-V mode by setting is_self_rlhfv=True . To ensure a fair comparison, we perform a small grid search over the safety trade-off coefficient lambda_value in {1, 10 , 20 } and report the best-performing configuration. The remaining reward-model hyperparameters are identical to those used in GRPO. 

## C Additional Experiment Results 

C.1 Ablations on RL Data and Mixed Teacher 

We ablate both the constitution ratio in RL data and the teacher configuration to test the hypothesis from our pilot study: safety OPSD collapse is driven primarily by the constitution-shaped teacher distribution, rather than by the data composition itself. We compare four settings in Table 4: (1) Data_C_OriTeacher , which applies constitutions to all safety-related samples and uses the original model as teacher; (2) Data_NonC15_OriTeacher , which randomly removes constitutions from 15% of safety samples while keeping the original teacher; (3) Data_NonC15_SFTTeacher ,which uses the same data split but replaces the teacher with the Cross-SFT calibrated teacher; and (4) Data_-NonC15_HybridTeacher , which uses the original model as the reverse-KL reference for constitution-masked samples and the SFT teacher for constitution-enabled samples. The results show that modifying the constitution ratio alone has limited effect. Compared with Data_C_-OriTeacher , Data_NonC15_OriTeacher only slightly improves helpfulness on BeaverTails-V and SPA-VL, while the overall safety–helpfulness trade-off remains poor. This supports our pilot-study conclusion that the collapse is not primarily data-induced: once the student is matched to an over-contracted teacher, reducing constitution exposure in a small portion of samples does not fundamentally change the optimization geometry. Finally, the mixed-teacher setting performs worse than using a single calibrated teacher. From an optimization perspective, this acts similarly to an additional KL-like constraint that slows the student’s movement toward the calibrated safety teacher, rather than providing a coherent alignment direction. The student fails to fully inherit either the safety boundary of the calibrated teacher or the expressiveness of the original model. A more principled treatment of mixed-reference distillation, especially under coupled safety–expressiveness geometry, is an interesting direction for future work. C.2 General Ability of OPSD Variants 

Table 5 shows that safety OPSD also incurs a noticeable alignment tax on general capabilities, especially on reasoning-intensive benchmarks such as MathVista and LLaVA-Wilder. In contrast, the degradation on standard VQA benchmarks is relatively smaller. This pattern is consistent with our pilot study: once constitutional conditioning contracts the teacher distribution, reverse KL does not only suppress unsafe behavior, but also leaks into the expressiveness dimension that supports complex reasoning. Among the variants, OPSD (Constitution) preserves the strongest overall performance, but still exhibits clear degradation on several benchmarks, indicating that the safety–expressiveness coupling is only partially alleviated. Self-Distilled RL with Outcome Rewards suffers the largest drop on harder multimodal reasoning tasks, while Entropy-Aware OPSD better preserves some short-form QA performance but remains unstable on compositional reasoning benchmarks. Overall, these results further support our claim that the collapse of safety OPSD extends beyond helpfulness reduction and manifests as a broader loss of expressive reasoning capacity.
